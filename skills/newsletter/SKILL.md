# Newsletter

Write a newsletter edition for: $ARGUMENTS

## Newsletter Tone

The newsletter reads like an engineer telling a friend what they've been working on. Not a thought leader explaining concepts to an audience.

**Sound like this:**
- "I've been using X and it's working really well. Here's the setup."
- "Before this, I was doing Y. It worked for a while but fell apart when..."
- "Not everything needs this. Small stuff I still just do the simple way."

**Don't sound like this:**
- "There's a better way." (infomercial)
- "Here's the bigger picture." (keynote)
- "That question is the whole game." (LinkedIn guru)
- "The real payoff is when you start..." (sales pitch)

### Key principles:

1. **Assume zero context.** The reader hasn't watched the video, hasn't heard of the tool, and doesn't know why they should care. Before showing any config or code, explain what the tool is (one sentence), what the feature does (one sentence), and why it matters (one sentence). If you mention a product, concept, or acronym for the first time, define it in plain language right there.

2. **Value up front.** Show the code, the config, the setup within the first few scrolls. Theory and context come after they've seen the thing working.

3. **First person, sharing experience.** "I use this" not "You need this." Describe what you do and let the reader decide if it applies to them.

4. **No named frameworks.** Don't label things "Level 1, Level 2, Level 3" or create branded concepts. Just describe what happened and what works.

5. **Don't defend or justify.** If it works, you don't need to explain why it's not over-engineering or unnecessary process. Just show it.

6. **Concrete examples.** Include actual code blocks, config files, commands, ticket examples. The more tangible and copy-pasteable, the better.

7. **Keep it tight.** Every paragraph should either show something useful or explain something the reader needs to understand. Cut anything that's just there to sound insightful.

8. **Simple language.** Don't try to sound clever or polished. "I started by staying pretty hands-on" beats "The key insight: once you build confidence in your specs, you can progressively delegate more."

## Structure

1. **Short intro** - 2-3 sentences max. What you've been doing, what you're going to show.
2. **The setup** - Code, config, commands. The tangible stuff. This should be the bulk of the post.
3. **Context** - Why you moved to this approach. Keep it brief, personal, first person.
4. **How you use it** - Practical notes from experience. What works, what doesn't.
5. **Try this** - One clear action they can take.

## Two modes

**Companion mode** - Write a newsletter that complements a YouTube video
- Reference the video
- Go deeper on one aspect
- Provide the "read" version for those who prefer text

**Original mode** - Standalone newsletter on a topic
- Full value in the email itself
- Can tease upcoming content

## Anti-patterns

- Long theoretical intros before showing anything useful
- Named frameworks and levels (Level 1, Level 2, Level 3)
- "The shift" / "The bigger picture" / "Here's what this unlocks" framing
- Defending against criticisms nobody made
- Dramatic one-liner closers trying to sound profound
- Bullet points that restate the same idea in slightly different words
- Anything that sounds like it belongs on a conference stage
- Em dashes. Use periods, commas, or rewrite the sentence.
