# Writing the 30-Second Intro

## What This Is

The first 30 seconds of the video. 4-8 sentences spoken aloud. This is where the viewer decides to stay or leave.

The intro has three beats. Each beat is 1-2 sentences. The beats should flow naturally — the viewer shouldn't feel structure, just momentum.

---

## The Psychology

People stay when they believe the video will give them something they want. The strongest intros frame a **dream outcome** delivered by a **relatable character** with **no special conditions**.

- **Dream outcome:** Not the problem — the desire. "A workflow where agents handle tickets end-to-end" not "managing agents is a mess."
- **Relatable character:** Someone the viewer sees themselves in. No flex, no unfair advantages.
- **No conditions:** The method feels achievable. "One pattern" not "after years of distributed systems experience."

Lead with desire, not pain. The problem can appear in the bridge, but the opening line is the dream.

---

## Three Beats

### Beat 1: Hook (1-2 sentences)

Open with the dream outcome. This is the line that stops the scroll.

Pick a framing:

| Framing | When to use | Template |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **I did** | You have a real result to show | I [achieved outcome] using [method] |
| **I would** | Hypothetical or "start from scratch" angle | If I had to [scenario], I'd [method] |
| **You can** | Direct viewer address, broad appeal | If you want [outcome], [method] |
| **Can you** | The answer is surprising | Can you [achieve outcome] under [conditions]? |
| **They did** | Case study, third-party proof | [Person/team] just [achieved outcome] with [method] |
| **Question-stacking** | Topic has many open questions the viewer wants answered | [5-8 specific unanswered questions in the first 60 seconds] |
| **Contrarian lead** | You're going against conventional wisdom — or your own interest | I [built/used X] and I'll be the first to tell you [contrarian position] |

"I did" and "You can" are the workhorses. "They did" for case studies. "Can you" when the answer defies expectations. "I would" for opinionated takes.

**Question-stacking** works when the topic has genuine unresolved questions. List 5-8 specific questions rapid-fire in the first 60 seconds. Each question is a micro-curiosity loop — the viewer stays to get ALL answers, not just one. Don't ask vague questions ("is it any good?") — ask specific ones the viewer has already been wondering about ("can it handle multi-file edits across a monorepo?"). The questions become the implicit structure of the video.

**Contrarian lead** works when the contrarian position IS the thesis, not just an end-of-video honest take. "Going against your own interest" is especially powerful — e.g., "I built X and I'll be the first to tell you it's overkill now." The viewer trusts you immediately because you have nothing to gain from this position. Thread the contrarian stance from the hook through the entire video, not just one section.

### Beat 2: Bridge (1-2 sentences)

Context and credibility. Why should they trust this? Not a credential dump — woven into the story.

Ways to bridge:

- **Experience:** "I've been running this on real projects for three months and..."
- **Specificity:** "It handles ticket assignment, implementation, tests, and opens the PR — no intervention."
- **Contrast:** "Most tutorials show you the basics. This is what production actually looks like."
- **Stakes:** "This changed how I think about building software entirely."
- **Honesty:** "It's not perfect yet, but the workflow is solid and I'll show you exactly what works."

### Beat 3: Promise (1-2 sentences)

What the viewer gets. Be concrete.

- "Let me show you how to set this up."
- "I'll walk you through the whole system and you can clone the repo yourself."
- "I'll break down what it is, show you a live demo, and give you everything you need to get started."

## Example

**Title:** "I Replaced My Entire Dev Workflow with AI Agents"

> I just shipped a full feature — from ticket to merged PR — without writing a single line of code myself. The agents picked up the ticket, read the spec, implemented the changes, ran the tests, and opened the pull request. I've been running this workflow on real client projects for about three months now, and it's genuinely changed how I think about building software. Let me show you exactly how it works.

Beat 1 (hook): Dream outcome — shipped a feature without coding. "I did" framing.
Beat 2 (bridge): Specificity of what the agents do + credibility through real usage.
Beat 3 (promise): "Let me show you exactly how it works."

Notice: no "hey guys," no credentials, no stalling. Just straight into the result.

---

## Example: Question-Stacking

**Title:** "OpenClaw: Open Source Claw Machine"

> So I've been building an open-source claw machine and I have a LOT of questions. Can you actually control a claw machine over the internet with acceptable latency? Can a $12 servo grip a stuffed animal reliably? What happens when two people try to control it at the same time? Is the camera feed going to lag so badly it's unplayable? Can you run the whole thing on a Raspberry Pi? And the big one — will people actually play it? I've been working on this for three months and I have answers to all of these. Let me show you what I found.

Beat 1 (hook): 6 specific questions rapid-fire. Each one is a curiosity loop.
Beat 2 (bridge): "Three months" — credibility through real work.
Beat 3 (promise): "I have answers to all of these."

The viewer stays because they want ALL the answers, not just one.

---

## Example: Contrarian Lead

**Title:** "Claude Code Is All You Need"

> I've been building AI tools for two years now. Custom agents, orchestration layers, retrieval pipelines — the whole stack. And I'll be the first to tell you: for most of what I was building, Claude Code is all you actually need. I'm basically arguing against my own consulting business here, but it's true. Most teams don't need a custom agent framework. They need a good prompt and a tool that already exists. Let me show you what I mean and where the line actually is.

Beat 1 (hook): Contrarian position stated immediately — against his own interest.
Beat 2 (bridge): "Arguing against my own consulting business" — instant trust.
Beat 3 (promise): "Where the line actually is" — nuance, not absolutism.

## Rules

- **First sentence validates the title.** They clicked because of the title. Confirm immediately.
- **Be specific.** "A better workflow" is vague. "A workflow where agents pick up tickets and open PRs" is specific.
- **Every sentence earns its place.** If it doesn't hook, bridge, or promise — cut it.
- **No stalling.** Don't open with "Hey guys," "Before we get started," "In today's video." Just start.
- **Desire over problem.** Lead with where they want to be, not where they are.
- **Credential by showing, not telling.** "I've been doing this for six months and..." not "I'm a senior engineer with 20 years..."
- **Keep conditions low.** If the method sounds hard or requires special access, the viewer disengages. Frame the path as achievable.

---

## Voice

**Calm and authoritative.** Confidence from knowing the material, not from energy.

**Natural enthusiasm.** "This is really powerful" is fine. "This is INSANE" is not.

**Honest about limitations.** "These aren't the most mind-blowing results, but the pipeline is working."

**Engineering framing.** Tools are extensions of practice, not magic. "This is the same way we've always built software — we're just delegating to agents."

**Natural transitions are fine.** "Let's get into it," "so let's take a look," "let me show you" — these move things forward. The test: does it sound like you talking on camera? If yes, keep it.

**Doesn't fit:** "This is insane," "you're going to want to watch this," "smash that like button," "what's up everyone."

**The test:** Read the intro aloud. If it sounds like you explaining something to a colleague, it's right. If it sounds like a script, rewrite until the seams disappear.
